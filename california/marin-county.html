Curative Title Services in Marin County, California

Why Marin County Properties Face Title Challenges

Marin County is one of the most affluent and geographically distinctive counties in California. Bordered by the Pacific Ocean to the west, San Francisco Bay to the east, and home to the Golden Gate National Recreation Area, Point Reyes National Seashore, and Mount Tamalpais, the county combines extraordinary natural beauty with some of the highest property values in the nation. This combination of wealth, history, and geography creates a unique set of title challenges.

Much of Marin County's residential development occurred between the 1940s and 1970s, and many properties from this era carry title defects that have gone undetected for decades. Unreleased deeds of trust from lenders that no longer exist, incorrect legal descriptions based on outdated surveys, and boundary disputes between hillside properties with irregular lot lines are all common issues. The county's strict land use regulations — including extensive Coastal Zone restrictions and environmental protections — add recorded encumbrances that complicate title analysis.

Marin's demographics contribute to its title challenges as well. The county has one of the oldest median-age populations in the Bay Area, and the passing of long-time homeowners frequently triggers probate title issues, especially when properties were held in outdated trusts or when owners died without updated estate plans. Additionally, the significant number of properties held by family trusts, LLCs, and other entities requires careful examination to verify that all transfers were properly authorized and recorded. Redwood Title Solutions brings the precision and expertise required to resolve these layered title issues across Marin County.

California Title Law: What Marin County Property Owners Need to Know

Resolving title issues in Marin County requires deep understanding of California state law and the county's specific regulatory environment.

Tax Lien Procedures

Under the California Revenue and Taxation Code, property taxes are assessed annually and become liens on January 1. Delinquent taxes trigger penalties and interest, and after five years of default, the Marin County Tax Collector may sell the property at a tax-defaulted auction under Sections 3691-3731. Given Marin's exceptionally high property values, tax lien amounts can be substantial, and timely redemption is critical to preserving ownership.

Probate and Trust Administration

California Probate Code governs both traditional probate and trust administration. For Marin County properties held in revocable living trusts — which is common given the county's estate planning-conscious population — trust administration after the settlor's death follows Probate Code Division 9. Properties that pass outside of a trust require formal probate under Division 7, which in Marin County typically takes 12 to 18 months. Small estate affidavits under Probate Code Section 13100 provide an expedited alternative for qualifying estates.

Coastal Zone Considerations

Properties within Marin County's Coastal Zone are subject to the California Coastal Act (Public Resources Code Sections 30000-30900). Coastal Development Permits and related conditions may be recorded as encumbrances on title, and unpermitted development can create clouds that must be addressed before title can be insured. These regulatory encumbrances require specialized knowledge to navigate during the curative process.

Quiet Title and Recording

Quiet title actions in Marin County are filed under California Code of Civil Procedure Sections 760.010-764.080. As a race-notice recording state, California gives priority to interests that are both recorded first and obtained without notice of prior unrecorded claims. Comprehensive searching of Marin County records is essential to identify all potential clouds before pursuing curative action.
