Probate Title Clearing

When a property owner dies without a will, without completing probate, or with a flawed estate plan, the title to their real estate becomes trapped. No title company will insure it, no lender will finance it, and no buyer can confidently close on it until the probate-related clouds are resolved.

Probate title issues are among the most complex and emotionally sensitive challenges in curative title work. They involve family dynamics, legal proceedings that can span months or years, and state-specific intestate succession laws that determine who inherits and in what proportions. The intersection of family dynamics, estate procedures, and property ownership creates a web of requirements that must be carefully navigated to produce clear, insurable title.

At Redwood Title Solutions, we bring deep expertise in probate title clearing across California, Texas, and Montana. Each of these states has fundamentally different probate systems. California follows the California Probate Code and is a community property state. Texas operates under the Texas Estates Code with unique independent administration procedures. Montana follows the Uniform Probate Code, which offers streamlined informal probate options. Understanding these distinctions is essential to developing an efficient resolution strategy.

Whether you are an heir trying to sell inherited property, a personal representative seeking to administer an estate, or a title company needing assistance with a probate-clouded title, we have the expertise to identify the issues, determine the most efficient curative path, and execute the resolution. Our goal is to clear the title while respecting the legal rights of all parties and minimizing the time and cost required to reach a clean closing.

How It Works

Probate Laws by State

Probate procedures, intestate succession rules, and community property laws vary significantly between California, Texas, and Montana. Here is how each state handles probate-related title issues.

California

California is a community property state governed by the California Probate Code. Property acquired during marriage is presumed to be owned equally by both spouses. When one spouse dies, only their half of the community property passes through probate — the surviving spouse already owns the other half by operation of law.

Formal probate in California is required for estates exceeding $184,500 in value. The process typically takes 12-18 months and involves court supervision, notice to creditors, and inventory and appraisal of assets. California also offers the Independent Administration of Estates Act (IAEA), which gives executors broader authority to act without court confirmation.

For title clearing purposes, California provides several alternatives to full probate, including spousal property petitions under Probate Code Section 13500, small estate affidavits for personal property, and Heggstad petitions to confirm property held in trusts. Heirship affidavits are less commonly accepted by California title underwriters, making formal probate or court proceedings the typical requirement for clearing title.

Texas

Texas is also a community property state and operates under the Texas Estates Code. The state is notable for its independent administration system, which allows executors and administrators to manage estates with minimal court oversight. This is by far the most common form of probate in Texas and significantly reduces the time and cost of estate administration.

When a person dies intestate in Texas, the intestate succession rules determine inheritance. Texas law draws important distinctions between community property and separate property, and between real and personal property, when determining shares. A surviving spouse does not automatically inherit all community property — the deceased spouse's share may pass to children or other heirs.

Texas also allows small estate affidavits under Estates Code Section 205, which can transfer title without full probate when the estate meets certain requirements. Additionally, Texas title companies widely accept affidavits of heirship signed by disinterested witnesses as a basis for insuring title, particularly when combined with sufficient supporting evidence. This makes heirship affidavits a powerful and cost-effective tool for clearing probate- related title clouds in Texas.

Montana

Montana is a Uniform Probate Code (UPC) state, governed by Montana Code Annotated Title 72. The UPC provides a streamlined, flexible framework for estate administration that includes both formal and informal probate options. Informal probate is an administrative process that does not require a court hearing and can often be completed in 4-6 months.

Montana is not a community property state — it follows an equitable distribution model. This means that property ownership during marriage is determined by title, not by presumption of equal ownership. When a spouse dies intestate, the surviving spouse's share depends on whether the decedent had children from another relationship and the overall family structure.

Montana also recognizes affidavits of heirship for title clearing purposes, and the state's personal representative appointment process under the UPC is relatively straightforward. However, Montana's rural character and large properties can present unique challenges, including mineral rights reservations, water rights, and agricultural lease issues that complicate probate title clearing.

Common Scenarios

Probate title problems come in many forms. Here are the situations we encounter most frequently.

Multiple Generations Without Probate

A family property has passed through two or three generations without any formal probate or title transfer. The original owner died decades ago, and their children — some of whom have also died — are the record owners. The current occupants are grandchildren or great-grandchildren with no documented ownership interest. The chain of title is broken in multiple places, and dozens of potential heirs may have fractional interests. We conduct thorough genealogical and title research to identify all potential heirs, then determine the most efficient curative path, whether through probate proceedings, heirship affidavits, or quiet title action.

Surviving Spouse Cannot Sell Community Property

In California or Texas, a spouse dies and the surviving spouse attempts to sell the marital home. The title company discovers that the deed vested title in both spouses, and no probate was opened to transfer the deceased spouse's interest. Even though the surviving spouse occupied the property for years, they cannot convey clear title without first establishing their right to the deceased spouse's share. Depending on the state and circumstances, we may pursue a spousal property petition, a small estate affidavit, or a streamlined probate to clear the title.

Contested Will with Real Property

A decedent left a will that devises real property to one heir, but other family members contest the will's validity. The property cannot be sold or refinanced during the will contest because the rightful owner is in dispute. The title company will not insure until the court resolves the challenge. We work with the parties' attorneys to monitor the litigation and prepare all title documentation needed to clear the exceptions immediately upon resolution, minimizing delays once the court issues its final order.

Heir Located Out of State or Unresponsive

An estate has been probated, but one of the heirs lives out of state, is unresponsive, or cannot be located. Their signature is needed on a deed or other conveyance document to complete the title transfer. Without their cooperation, the transaction stalls. We conduct heir location research, coordinate with the personal representative and probate attorney, and explore alternative curative approaches — including partition actions or court-ordered sales — to resolve the impasse and deliver insurable title.

Our Resolution Process

Our probate title clearing process is structured to address every layer of complexity while minimizing time and cost.

1

Title & Probate Research

We begin with a comprehensive title search to establish the current chain of title and identify where the breaks occurred. Simultaneously, we search probate court records to determine whether any probate proceedings were filed, whether letters of administration or testamentary were issued, and whether any court orders affect the property. We also review vital records to establish the family tree and identify all potential heirs.

2

Heir Identification & Outreach

Using genealogical research, public records databases, and investigative techniques, we identify and locate all heirs with potential interests in the property. We determine each heir's fractional share under the applicable state's intestate succession laws and initiate contact to inform them of their interests and the curative process.

3

Curative Strategy Selection

Based on the research, we select the most efficient curative approach. Options include formal probate, small estate affidavits, heirship affidavits, spousal property petitions, Heggstad petitions (California), independent administration (Texas), informal probate (Montana), or quiet title action. We present the recommended strategy with estimated timelines and costs.

4

Document Coordination & Professional Management

We coordinate the preparation of all necessary documents — heirship affidavits, deeds, and supporting exhibits — working with probate attorneys who handle court petitions and filings. For heirship affidavits, we identify and coordinate with disinterested witnesses. For court proceedings, we ensure the attorneys we work with have everything they need to move efficiently.

5

Title Clearance & Underwriter Approval

Once the curative documents are recorded or the court orders are issued, we work with the title company and underwriter to confirm that all probate-related exceptions have been removed from the title commitment. We provide a complete documentation package and coordinate directly with the underwriter to obtain final approval for insurable title.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens to property title when someone dies without a will?

When a person dies intestate (without a will), their property passes according to the state's intestate succession laws. These laws establish a hierarchy of heirs — typically starting with a surviving spouse and children, then parents, siblings, and more distant relatives. Until a court proceeding or legally sufficient affidavit establishes who the rightful heirs are and in what proportions they inherit, the title remains clouded and cannot be transferred or insured.

What is an heirship affidavit and when is it needed?

An heirship affidavit is a sworn legal document that identifies the heirs of a deceased property owner. It is typically signed by two or more disinterested parties — people who are not heirs themselves but have personal knowledge of the decedent's family history and marital status. In Texas and Montana, properly executed heirship affidavits are widely accepted by title underwriters as a basis for insuring title without requiring full probate. In California, heirship affidavits are less commonly accepted, and court proceedings are usually required.

How long does the probate process take for real estate?

Timelines vary significantly by state. In California, formal probate typically takes 12-18 months due to court supervision requirements and mandatory creditor notice periods. In Texas, independent administration can often be completed in 6-9 months. Montana's Uniform Probate Code allows informal probate, which can be completed in as little as 4-6 months. Cases involving will contests, missing heirs, or creditor disputes may take considerably longer in any state.

Can property be sold during probate?

Yes, in most circumstances. In California, the personal representative can sell property during probate, but court confirmation may be required unless independent administration authority was granted. In Texas, an independent executor can generally sell estate property without court approval, subject to any restrictions in the will. In Montana, personal representatives appointed under the UPC can sell property as part of estate administration. The title company will require specific probate documentation before insuring the sale.

What is the small estate procedure and does it apply to real estate?

Small estate procedures provide simplified alternatives to full probate for estates below certain value thresholds. In California, the small estate affidavit (Probate Code Section 13100) applies only to personal property under $184,500 — real property generally requires formal probate or a specialized petition. Texas allows small estate affidavits that can cover real property under certain conditions. Montana permits summary administration under the Uniform Probate Code for qualifying small estates. The applicability of these procedures depends on the specific facts of each case.

What are heir property issues and how do they affect title?

Heir property refers to real estate that has been passed down through generations without formal probate or title transfer, resulting in fractional ownership among many heirs. This is particularly common in families where property was held for decades and multiple owners have died without wills. Over time, the number of co-owners can grow into the dozens. This creates severe title problems because all co-owners technically must agree to any sale or encumbrance, and locating and obtaining cooperation from all heirs can be extremely challenging.

What is the difference between community property and separate property in probate?

In community property states like California and Texas, property acquired during marriage is generally owned equally by both spouses, regardless of whose name is on the title. When one spouse dies, only their half of the community property passes through probate. Separate property — acquired before marriage, by gift, or by inheritance — belongs entirely to the individual spouse and passes fully through their estate. This distinction is critical for probate title clearing because it determines what interests actually need to be transferred and who has legal standing to convey them.

Do I need a probate attorney to clear title on inherited property?

If court proceedings are required, yes — a probate attorney is necessary to represent the estate or petitioner. However, not all probate title issues require court proceedings. Heirship affidavits, for example, can often be prepared and recorded without litigation. At Redwood Title Solutions, we handle the research and coordination aspects: chain of title research, heir identification, locating disinterested witnesses, and coordinating with title companies. We work alongside probate attorneys when court proceedings are needed, ensuring efficient collaboration between all parties involved in the resolution.
