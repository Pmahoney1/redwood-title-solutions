Tax Lien Resolution

Unpaid property taxes create one of the most common and most urgent clouds on title. Whether the issue involves delinquent county taxes, an IRS federal tax lien, or a state tax lien from a prior owner, the result is the same: the property cannot be sold, refinanced, or insured until the lien is resolved.

Tax lien resolution sits at the intersection of property tax regulations, state-specific procedures, and title insurance underwriting. Each state handles tax delinquency differently, with distinct redemption periods, sale procedures, priority rules, and statutory requirements. A resolution strategy that works in California may be entirely inapplicable in Texas or Montana, and missteps can result in the permanent loss of property rights.

At Redwood Title Solutions, we specialize in clearing tax-related title defects across California, Texas, and Montana. Our team understands the nuances of each state's Revenue and Taxation Code, Tax Code, and Montana Code Annotated provisions governing tax lien attachment, priority, and redemption. We work methodically through county tax collector offices, the IRS, and state taxing authorities to identify, negotiate, and resolve every tax-related cloud on your title.

Whether you are dealing with years of accumulated delinquent taxes, a property that has already gone to tax auction, or a federal tax lien recorded against a prior owner, we have the expertise to chart the most efficient path to clear, insurable title. Our goal is always the same: remove the cloud, satisfy the underwriter, and position the property for a clean closing.

How It Works

Tax Lien Rules by State

Tax lien attachment, priority, and redemption differ dramatically between California, Texas, and Montana. Understanding these differences is critical to developing an effective resolution strategy.

California

California operates under a tax deed system governed by the Revenue and Taxation Code. When property taxes go unpaid, the property becomes "tax-defaulted" and the county tax collector records a notice of default. The owner has a five-year redemption period from the date of default to pay the delinquent taxes, penalties, and interest.

If the property is not redeemed within five years, the tax collector may initiate a public auction under Revenue and Taxation Code sections 3691-3731. California tax sales transfer title via a tax deed, which extinguishes most prior liens. However, IRS federal tax liens survive for 120 days after the sale unless separately addressed.

Property tax liens in California are given first-priority status, ahead of virtually all other encumbrances including mortgages and judgment liens. The state also imposes penalties of 10% on the first installment if delinquent, plus additional penalties and costs that accrue over time, making early resolution significantly less expensive than waiting.

Texas

Texas operates under a significantly different framework governed by the Texas Tax Code. Texas is a non-judicial foreclosure state for tax liens, meaning the taxing authority can pursue a tax sale through a relatively expedited court process. The redemption periods are notably shorter than California's.

For homestead and agricultural properties, the former owner has just six months after the tax sale to redeem the property. For all other property types, the redemption period extends to two years. During the redemption period, the original owner must pay the purchase price plus a 25% premium (for the six-month period) or a 50% premium (for the two-year period).

Texas also allows multiple taxing units (county, city, school district, special districts) to combine their claims into a single tax suit, which can result in substantial aggregate delinquent amounts. The state's strong homestead protections add another layer of complexity, as homestead properties receive different treatment in both the foreclosure and redemption processes.

Montana

Montana's tax lien process is governed by Montana Code Annotated Title 15. When property taxes become delinquent, the county treasurer issues a tax lien certificate. The property owner has a three-year redemption period from the date of the tax lien certificate to pay the delinquent taxes, penalties, interest, and costs.

If the property is not redeemed within three years, the tax lien purchaser (or the county, if the certificate was not sold) may apply for a tax deed. Montana requires that the applicant for a tax deed provide notice to the property owner and all parties with a recorded interest, creating an opportunity for last-minute redemption.

Montana's rural character means that many tax-delinquent properties involve large acreages, mineral rights complications, and water rights considerations. The interaction between surface rights, mineral reservations, and tax lien priority can be particularly complex. Our team has deep experience navigating these Montana-specific issues.

Common Scenarios

Tax lien title issues arise in a variety of real-world situations. Here are some of the most common scenarios we handle at Redwood Title Solutions.

Inherited Property with Years of Delinquent Taxes

A property owner passes away and their heirs inherit the property but are unaware of — or unable to pay — the accumulating property taxes. By the time the heirs attempt to sell or refinance, multiple years of delinquent taxes, penalties, and interest have accumulated. The title company flags the delinquency and refuses to insure. We step in to calculate the total redemption amount, negotiate payment plans with the county when available, and coordinate payoff to clear the lien before the property reaches tax auction.

IRS Federal Tax Lien from a Prior Owner

A property was previously owned by someone who owed federal taxes. The IRS recorded a Notice of Federal Tax Lien against all of that person's property in the county, and the lien now appears on the current owner's title report. Even though the current owner is not the taxpayer, the lien attaches to the property and must be resolved. We work with the IRS to obtain a Certificate of Discharge, which removes the property from the lien while the IRS continues to pursue the taxpayer's other assets.

Property Sold at Tax Auction — Redemption Period Issues

A property went through a county tax sale, and the purchaser obtained either a tax lien certificate or a tax deed. However, questions remain about whether proper notice was given to the former owner and all interested parties, whether the redemption period has truly expired, or whether the tax sale itself was conducted in compliance with all statutory requirements. We audit the entire tax sale process, identify any defects, and either cure them or use them as leverage to negotiate a resolution that produces clear, insurable title.

State Tax Lien Competing with Mortgage Lien

A state taxing authority records a lien against a property for unpaid income or franchise taxes owed by the property owner. The lien competes with an existing mortgage for priority position. The title company needs clarity on which lien takes priority and whether the state lien will survive a foreclosure or sale. We research the recording dates, analyze the applicable state lien priority statutes, and negotiate with the state taxing authority to obtain either a full release or a subordination agreement that allows the transaction to close.

Our Resolution Process

Every tax lien resolution follows a structured process designed to identify all issues, develop the optimal strategy, and execute efficiently.

1

Comprehensive Tax Lien Analysis

We begin by pulling the full tax history from the county tax collector, searching for all recorded federal and state tax liens, and identifying every taxing authority with a claim against the property. This analysis reveals the total exposure, the priority of each lien, and the applicable redemption deadlines.

2

Strategy Development

Based on the analysis, we develop a resolution strategy tailored to the specific state and lien type. Options may include full redemption, negotiated payoff at a reduced amount, IRS discharge application, attorney-led quiet title action, or a combination of approaches. We present the options, estimated costs, and timelines before proceeding.

3

Negotiation & Payoff Coordination

We engage directly with county tax collectors, the IRS, and state taxing authorities to negotiate the resolution. For county taxes, we coordinate redemption payments and obtain certificates of redemption. For IRS liens, we coordinate discharge applications under Internal Revenue Code Section 6325. For state liens, we negotiate releases or subordination agreements.

4

Documentation & Recording

Once the liens are satisfied, we obtain and record all necessary release documents — certificates of redemption, IRS certificates of discharge, state lien releases, and reconveyance documents. We ensure every document is properly recorded in the correct county recorder's office.

5

Title Clearance Confirmation

After recording, we work with the title company and underwriter to confirm that all tax-related exceptions have been removed from the title commitment. We provide a complete resolution package documenting every step taken, ensuring the title is now fully insurable and ready for closing.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a tax lien on a property title?

A tax lien is a legal claim placed on a property by a government entity when property taxes go unpaid. It takes priority over most other liens and encumbrances, including mortgages and judgment liens. If the lien is not resolved, the taxing authority can ultimately force a sale of the property to recover the unpaid taxes. Tax liens appear on title reports and prevent the issuance of title insurance until resolved.

How long is the redemption period for tax-delinquent property in California?

In California, the redemption period is five years from the date the property becomes tax-defaulted. During this period, the property owner can pay the delinquent taxes, penalties, and interest to redeem the property and prevent it from being sold at a county tax auction. After the five-year period expires, the county tax collector may initiate a public auction under the Revenue and Taxation Code.

Can an IRS federal tax lien be removed from a property?

Yes. There are several methods for resolving an IRS federal tax lien on a property. The most common approaches include full payment of the underlying tax debt, obtaining a Certificate of Discharge (which releases the specific property from the lien), lien subordination (which allows a mortgage to take priority), or waiting for the 10-year collection statute of limitations to expire. Each method has specific IRS requirements and processing timelines. We regularly coordinate IRS discharge applications for our clients.

What happens to a property after a tax auction in Texas?

After a tax auction in Texas, the former owner retains a right of redemption. For homestead and agricultural properties, the redemption period is six months. For all other property types, the redemption period is two years. To redeem, the former owner must pay the purchaser the amount paid at the sale plus a premium — 25% for the six-month redemption and 50% for the two-year redemption. If the property is not redeemed, the purchaser receives a tax deed conveying full title.

How does a tax lien affect the ability to sell or refinance a property?

A tax lien creates a cloud on title that prevents the issuance of title insurance. Since virtually all real estate transactions and refinancing require title insurance, an unresolved tax lien effectively blocks any sale or refinance. Lenders will not fund a loan on a property with outstanding tax liens, and buyers cannot obtain the title insurance protection they need. The lien must be fully resolved and released before the title company will remove the exception and insure the title.

What is the difference between a tax lien and a tax deed?

A tax lien is a claim against property for unpaid taxes — it does not transfer ownership. A tax deed is a conveyance document issued after a tax sale or foreclosure that transfers actual ownership of the property. Some states conduct tax lien sales (selling the right to collect the debt), while others conduct tax deed sales (selling the property itself). California and Montana use tax deed sales, while the process varies by jurisdiction. The distinction matters greatly for title resolution because the legal rights and remedies differ depending on which mechanism was used.

How long does it take to resolve a tax lien on a property title?

Timelines vary significantly. Simple delinquent property tax issues where the solution is straightforward redemption may resolve in 30-60 days. IRS federal tax lien discharge applications typically take 3-6 months to process. Cases involving tax auction redemption rights, defective tax sale procedures, or the need for a quiet title action may take 6-12 months or longer. We provide realistic timeline estimates after our initial analysis and keep you informed of progress throughout the process.
