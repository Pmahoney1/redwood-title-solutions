Title Search & Opinion

Every sound real estate transaction begins with a thorough title search. Without a clear understanding of who owns the property, what encumbrances burden it, and what defects exist in the chain of title, the risks of closing are incalculable. A comprehensive title search is not just a formality — it is the diagnostic foundation upon which all curative work is built.

A title search is a systematic examination of public records to trace the complete history of ownership for a specific property. It involves reviewing every recorded deed, mortgage, lien, easement, restriction, court order, and other instrument that has been filed against the property in the county recorder's office, tax assessor's office, and relevant courts. The goal is to produce a comprehensive picture of the current state of the title — who owns it, what encumbers it, and what must be resolved before it can be insured and transferred.

At Redwood Title Solutions, we go far beyond standard title searches. Our curative title searches are designed to identify not just the obvious clouds — active liens and recorded encumbrances — but also the subtle defects that standard searches often miss: gaps in the chain of title, deed irregularities, vesting inconsistencies, probate issues, unresolved estates, and recording errors buried deep in the property's history. We search county records, probate court files, tax rolls, judgment indices, federal tax lien records, and corporate filings to build a complete picture.

Once the search is complete, we provide a detailed title analysis that classifies every finding, assesses the risk and severity of each issue, and recommends specific curative actions. For clients who need a formal attorney opinion letter, we can connect you with qualified real estate attorneys in each state who produce opinion letters that satisfy lender and underwriter requirements. Our title search and analysis service is the starting point for virtually every engagement we accept.

How It Works

Title Search Systems by State

Each state maintains its own recording systems and title plant structures. Understanding how records are organized and accessed in each state is essential to conducting a thorough search.

California

California uses a grantor-grantee index system maintained by each county recorder's office. Records are indexed by the names of the parties to each instrument — the grantor (seller/transferor) and the grantee (buyer/transferee). To trace the chain of title, the searcher must work backward through the grantee index to find each prior transfer, then forward through the grantor index to verify that the property was not conveyed to someone else.

California also has well-developed title plants maintained by major title companies. These private databases compile and index county records by property (tract/lot or assessor parcel number), making searches faster and more reliable. However, for curative work involving older records, probate files, or unusual instruments, direct examination of county records remains essential.

California's 58 counties have varying levels of digitization. Some counties have fully digital records going back decades; others require in-person examination of physical record books for older documents. Our team has experience searching records in every California county we serve, both online and in person at the recorder's office.

Texas

Texas also uses a grantor-grantee index system, with records maintained by each of the state's 254 county clerks. Texas has more counties than any other state, and recording practices, indexing quality, and digital availability vary considerably from county to county. Urban counties like Harris (Houston) and Dallas have sophisticated electronic recording systems, while rural counties may have more limited digital access.

Texas has a robust title plant industry, with title companies maintaining extensive private databases indexed by property. These plants are essential for efficient searching, but they do not capture all relevant records — particularly probate files, judgment indices, and federal tax lien records, which must be searched separately.

A unique aspect of Texas title work is the importance of survey records. Many Texas properties, particularly in rural areas, are described by metes and bounds referenced to original Spanish, Mexican, or Republic of Texas land grants and surveys. Understanding the survey system and the chain of title from the original grant is sometimes necessary for thorough title research, especially for properties with boundary disputes or overlapping claims.

Montana

Montana maintains recording offices in each of its 56 counties, using a grantor-grantee and tract index system. Many Montana counties maintain tract indices that organize records by legal description (section, township, and range), making property-specific searches more straightforward than pure name-based searching.

Montana's title search landscape is shaped by its historical land use patterns. Many properties have roots in federal land patents — original grants from the U.S. government under the Homestead Act or mining laws. Tracing title back to the patent is sometimes necessary, particularly when mineral rights were reserved or when the original patent contained conditions or limitations that still affect the property.

The presence of mineral rights and water rights adds significant complexity to Montana title searches. Mineral rights may have been severed from the surface estate decades ago and conveyed separately multiple times. Water rights may be appurtenant to the land or may have been adjudicated, transferred, or abandoned. A thorough Montana title search must account for both the surface estate and any associated mineral and water rights to give a complete picture of the property.

Common Scenarios

Title search and opinion services are needed in a wide range of situations. Here are some of the most common.

Pre-Transaction Due Diligence

Before committing to a complex property transaction, the parties need a clear-eyed assessment of the title's condition. A standard title commitment identifies exceptions, but it does not explain the severity of each issue, the cost and difficulty of resolution, or the risk of proceeding with unresolved defects. Our curative title search provides the deep analysis needed to make informed decisions — identifying every cloud, assessing the difficulty of each cure, and estimating the time and cost to achieve insurable title. This allows parties to negotiate with full knowledge of the title condition.

Title Commitment Review and Exception Analysis

A title company has issued a commitment with numerous Schedule B-II exceptions — easements, restrictions, mineral reservations, and other items that will not be covered by the insurance policy. The parties need an independent review to determine which exceptions are standard and acceptable, which represent real risks, and which can be removed through curative action. We review each exception, pull and analyze the underlying documents, and provide a detailed analysis of the practical impact and insurability of each item.

Attorney Opinion Letter for Lender Requirements

A lender or special servicer requires a formal attorney title opinion letter before funding a loan or approving a transaction. The opinion must address the current ownership, all encumbrances, the priority of the proposed mortgage, and the insurability of the title. We conduct the underlying title research and prepare the analysis, then connect you with a qualified real estate attorney in the applicable state who produces a formal opinion letter that meets the lender's specific requirements and satisfies underwriting standards.

Curative Title Search for Properties with Known Issues

A property has known title problems — a prior probate that may not have been completed, an unreleased mortgage, multiple judgment liens, or a history of informal family transfers. A standard title search will identify these issues, but our curative search goes further: researching the probate court files, tracing the chain of lender assignments, pulling each judgment from the court records, and reconstructing the informal transfer history. The result is a comprehensive roadmap for resolution that identifies every step needed to achieve clear title.

Our Search & Opinion Process

Our title search and opinion process is designed to provide the deepest, most actionable analysis available.

1

Scope Definition

We begin by understanding the purpose of the search — transaction support, investment due diligence, estate settlement, or curative analysis — and define the appropriate scope. This includes the search period (how far back to go), the record sources to examine (county recorder, tax assessor, probate court, judgment index, federal records), and the deliverable format (summary report or detailed analysis).

2

Records Examination

Our title examiners conduct a systematic review of all relevant records. We search the grantor-grantee indices, tract indices, judgment indices, tax records, probate court files, federal tax lien records, and UCC filings. Every recorded instrument in the chain is pulled, reviewed, and analyzed. We create a detailed chain of title abstract documenting every transfer, encumbrance, and recorded instrument affecting the property.

3

Defect Identification & Analysis

We analyze the chain of title for every type of defect: gaps in ownership, deed errors, recording irregularities, vesting inconsistencies, unresolved liens, unreleased encumbrances, probate issues, easement conflicts, and boundary concerns. Each defect is classified by type, severity, and estimated difficulty of resolution. We also identify potential issues that may not yet be apparent but could surface during a transaction.

4

Title Analysis & Curative Recommendations

We prepare a comprehensive title analysis report that presents the current state of the title, lists all findings with supporting document references, and provides specific curative recommendations for each defect. The report includes estimated timelines and costs for each curative action, prioritized by urgency and complexity. When a formal attorney opinion letter is needed, we connect you with a state-licensed attorney.

5

Consultation & Next Steps

We review the findings with the client, answer questions, and help prioritize the curative actions. If the client elects to proceed with curative work, the title search and opinion serve as the foundation for our resolution strategy. We can seamlessly transition from the search phase into active curative work, with no duplication of effort or loss of institutional knowledge.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a title search?

A title search is a thorough examination of public records to establish the chain of ownership for a specific property and identify any liens, encumbrances, easements, restrictions, or defects affecting the title. It involves reviewing deeds, mortgages, court records, tax records, judgment indices, and other documents recorded against the property. A comprehensive title search is the foundation of every real estate transaction and the starting point for all curative title work.

What is a title opinion letter?

A title opinion letter is a formal written analysis prepared by a qualified title professional or real estate attorney. It evaluates the current state of a property's title based on the results of a title search, identifies the current owner of record, lists all encumbrances and exceptions, and provides a professional opinion on the marketability and insurability of the title. Title opinion letters are frequently required by lenders, title insurance underwriters, and parties to complex transactions as a condition of closing.

How far back does a title search go?

The depth of a title search depends on its purpose and the state. Standard searches for routine transactions typically cover 40-60 years or back to the last title insurance policy. In Texas, certain searches extend to the original land grant (the "sovereignty search"). In Montana, mineral rights issues may require searching back to the original federal patent. Our curative searches go as far back as necessary to identify and trace the root of any defect, which can sometimes mean examining records from the 19th century or earlier.

What is the difference between a title search and title insurance?

A title search is the research process — the examination of public records to determine the current state of the title. Title insurance is a policy that protects against financial loss from defects that were not discovered during the search or that were unknown at the time of the policy. The title search informs the insurance underwriter about what conditions exist, and the underwriter uses that information to determine what exceptions to include in the policy. Our role is to conduct thorough searches, identify all issues, and resolve defects so that clean title insurance can be issued with minimal exceptions.

What does a title commitment include?

A title commitment (also called a title binder or preliminary title report in California) has three main components. Schedule A identifies the proposed insured party, the type of policy, the legal description, and the current vested owner. Schedule B-I lists the requirements that must be satisfied before the policy can be issued, such as paying off existing liens, obtaining releases, or recording specific documents. Schedule B-II lists the exceptions — items that will not be covered by the policy, such as easements, recorded restrictions, mineral reservations, and standard exclusions.

How long does a title search take?

Timelines depend on the complexity of the property and the depth of search required. Standard searches for properties with clean, recent history typically take 3-7 business days. Properties with complex ownership histories, multiple recorded documents, or potential defects may require 2-4 weeks. Deep curative searches involving probate court research, multi-county investigations, or historical record examination may take 4-8 weeks. We provide realistic timeline estimates at the beginning of every engagement based on our preliminary assessment of the property.

What issues can a title search uncover?

A comprehensive title search can reveal a wide range of issues: tax liens, judgment liens, mechanic's liens, HOA liens, open mortgages and deeds of trust, easements and access restrictions, CC&Rs and use restrictions, boundary disputes and survey discrepancies, breaks in the chain of title, deed errors and recording defects, unresolved probate and estate issues, forged or fraudulent documents, mineral and water rights reservations, pending lawsuits affecting the property, and federal and state tax liens. Each issue requires specific analysis and curative action.

Do I need a title search if I already have title insurance?

Existing title insurance protects against defects that existed at the time the policy was issued. It does not cover new issues — liens recorded after the policy date, new judgments, transfers, or other changes. If you are selling, refinancing, or dealing with a title dispute, a new title search reflecting the current state of the records is essential. Even if your existing policy is still in effect, its exceptions may need to be reviewed and resolved for a new transaction. A current search ensures that all parties have accurate, up-to-date information about the title condition.
